Sometimes these are used to denote the psychologically most important or most prominent word (He is wrong / I am right), sometimes to denote what comes first (They saw him / She married him), sometimes what is already known, with what is less well known as the predicate (He was her brother), sometimes the doer (He was loved by his father), sometimes the word stressed (You can’t deceive me), etc. Used in these senses, a “psychological subject” is merely like a focussed point in a given picture, and it will never be possible to limit that focus to just any one word. Very often the whole sentence forms a unified concept with no central focus or with an even focus, where we should have to admit that the whole sentence is the subject, which would be absurd, grammatically speaking. Such problems belong more properly in the province of logic).

Kinds of Subject

12.20. Kinds of Subject.—The kinds of subject in the English language are represented by the following examples:—

(Noun) The garden is in bloom.
(Verbal N.) Fishing is a pleasant pastime.
(Pronoun) He was a fool. It is winter.
(Infinitive) To talk is easy; to act, difficult.
(Noun Clause) How he did it I can’t understand.
Whether he did it or not is none of my business.
That this is wrong is admitted.
Who breaks pays.
Who steals my purse steals trash.

These are the standard types.
